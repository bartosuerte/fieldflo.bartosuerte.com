## 3. Overall strategy, the positioning that makes you winnable

### The core reframe (memorize this)
> "I'm not a product designer who's learning AI. I'm an AI-native builder who does the work of product design, end to end, shipping real interfaces, and I've been doing that substance for fifteen years under different titles."

This flips every gap. You're not "under-qualified for senior product design." You're "the rare hire who already operates the way FieldFlō says it wants to operate," and the product-design vocabulary is the catch-up, not the AI fluency.

### Your three pillars (every answer should ladder back to one)
1. **AI-native for real.** You ship with Claude in hours, not slides about AI someday. This is the JD's #1 required trait and the one most candidates fake.
2. **Clarity from chaos.** Fifteen years turning vague briefs and messy systems into clean, on-brand, usable work, directly the JD's "translate messy real-world inputs into clear, opinionated interfaces" and "bring clarity to fragmented workflows."
3. **Ownership + bias to action.** Freelance means you own outcomes end-to-end with no hand-holding, the exact operating style under "Who Will Thrive Here."

### Lead with / neutralize / never say

**Lead with (spend your airtime here):**
- The Applicant Review Tool as a product-design walkthrough, not a portfolio piece, a proof of how you work.
- Concrete Claude workflow specifics (Cowork for concepting → Claude Code for the build → GitHub for versioning → your own design system).
- The AI-UX decisions you made in the tool (this is the bridge to "AI/probabilistic systems" experience, see §4 Q2).

**Neutralize (address briefly, honestly, then pivot, don't dwell):**
- B2B SaaS gap → acknowledge, then trade on AI-nativeness + ownership + speed of ramp.
- Domain gap → frame as advantage (fresh eyes, no legacy assumptions) plus a credible learning plan.
- Title gap → "different labels, same substance," backed by the tool.

**Never say (these sink you):**
- Don't claim B2B SaaS product experience you don't have. One specific follow-up and you're done, and this team values direct communication; a bluff reads as exactly the wrong culture signal.
- Don't over-apologize or list your gaps unprompted. Confidence is part of the test.
- Don't bad-mouth "traditional" designers, position yourself additively, not by tearing others down.
- Don't promise you'll "learn product design on the job" as if it's foreign, you've done it; sell the substance.

### The mindset
They literally wrote "if you want a perfectly conventional resume, I'm not your person." That's not bravado in your cover letter, it's a **culture-match signal** this specific company rewards. Underdogs lose when they try to look like the favorite. You win by being undeniably, specifically you: fast, honest, AI-native, allergic to fluff.

## 4. Interview questions & how to answer them

For each: what they're really testing, the trap, your strategy, and a sample answer in your voice. Adapt the wording, don't recite.

### A. The gap-probing questions (the dangerous ones)

---

**Q1. "Your background is creative and marketing, this is a senior product design role. Why should we think you can do it?"**

- Really testing: Self-awareness + whether you'll bluff.
- Trap: Getting defensive, or conceding too much ("you're right, I'm a stretch").
- Strategy: Name it confidently, then redefine the work in terms of substance and end on the AI edge.

> "Fair question, and I'll be straight: my titles say creative and web. But the work underneath has been product design for years, taking a vague brief, mapping the flow, making the hierarchy and default decisions, and shipping a real, usable interface end to end. The label is new; the substance isn't. What I'd add that's genuinely hard to hire for is that I already build the way you say you want to work, Claude-native, shipping in hours. Most senior product designers are retrofitting AI into their process. I started there."

---

**Q2. "We need someone who's designed for systems with AI or probabilistic components. Have you?"**

- Really testing: The required portfolio bullet, can you reason about AI/uncertainty in a product, not just use AI tools.
- Trap: Saying "no" and stopping. You actually have done this, in the tool.
- Strategy: Walk the AI-UX decisions in your Applicant Review Tool using their exact framing. This is your single most important answer, rehearse it cold.

> "Yes, in the Applicant Review Tool. It grades real candidate documents, which means dealing head-on with model uncertainty, and I made deliberate UX calls about it. I separated a deterministic, transparent score from Claude's qualitative read, and labeled the AI-generated parts explicitly so a recruiter always knows what's a hard signal versus a judgment call. When the model can't be reached, it fails honestly, it tells the user and falls back instead of pretending. And I kept a human in the loop with an 'Ask' box so the recruiter can interrogate the result rather than just trust it. Those are exactly the decisions in your JD, how suggestions are presented, how confidence and uncertainty are communicated, where humans stay in the loop. I've made them, on something live."

(This answer alone converts your "0 portfolio / no AI features" LinkedIn score into a direct hit on a required bullet. It's true, it's specific, and it's in the company's own language.)

---

**Q3. "You don't have B2B SaaS experience. Two years is a hard requirement for us. Talk to me."**

- Really testing: Honesty + whether your other strengths offset it.
- Trap: Inventing SaaS experience, or deflating.
- Strategy: Concede the literal gap in one sentence, reframe to adjacent strengths, close on ramp speed.

> "Straight answer, I haven't held a B2B SaaS product seat, so I won't pretend the two years is there. What I'd point to instead: I've worked inside compliance-sensitive, high-stakes environments, at Humble & Fume I owned communications in an investor-relations-heavy, regulated space, so 'decisions ripple into legal and financial risk' isn't abstract to me. And the thing that usually makes SaaS onboarding slow is ramp speed and ownership. I ramp in days because I build to learn, and I own outcomes without hand-holding. I'd rather show you I'm productive in week one than claim a credential I don't have."

---

**Q4. "Walk me through your product design portfolio."**

- Really testing: Depth of design thinking across real work.
- Trap: Apologizing for thin volume.
- Strategy: Quality over quantity. Go deep on ONE thing (the tool) as a full design walkthrough, then reference breadth (15 yrs of shipped client work, the Humble & Fume systems redesign).
- Walkthrough spine to rehearse: **Problem** (let a hiring team grade any applicant fairly against a posting) → **Users** (recruiter + applicant) → **Key decisions** (six role-derived categories, transparent scoring, evidence pulled from the candidate's own words) → **AI-UX decisions** (Q2 above) → **Tradeoffs** (deterministic vs generative; free/local vs live-AI) → **Outcome & next** (shipped, live, in use; what you'd add with more time). Then: "Underneath that is fifteen years of shipping client sites end to end and one full systems redesign at Humble & Fume, happy to go there too."

---

**Q5. "This domain, demolition, asbestos abatement, environmental remediation, is brutally complex and you have zero exposure. Concern?"**

- Really testing: Humility + whether you'll drown, or learn fast.
- Trap: Faking domain knowledge.
- Strategy: Turn fresh eyes into an asset, pair with a credible 30-day learning plan, and show you've already started.

> "I'd flip it: I've spent a career walking into industries I didn't start in and getting fluent fast enough to design for them, that's the freelance job. Coming in without legacy assumptions is part of why the interface gets clearer, not messier. I'd spend my first weeks shadowing real field and office users, reading your compliance flows, and mapping the field-to-finance lifecycle before I touch pixels. I've already read up on FieldFlō and the abatement workflow to prep for today, clarity in regulated, real-world processes is the exact instinct I bring."

---

**Q6. "How would you handle being one of very few designers, with little structure?"** (the "2nd/3rd designer" preferred bullet)

- Really testing: Autonomy, ambiguity tolerance.
- Strategy: This is home turf for a 15-year freelancer. Don't hedge.

> "That's just Tuesday for me. Freelance is being the whole design org, strategy, decisions, build, ship, own the result. I set my own priorities and create the structure that doesn't exist yet. Partnering with a Lead Product Designer would actually be a step up in support from how I usually operate."
