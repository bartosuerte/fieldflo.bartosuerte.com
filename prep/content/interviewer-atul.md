## 6. What repels Atul, the traps this interviewer creates

1. **Fluffing or bluffing, fatal.** He maps flows and reasons about feasibility; inflated claims die on the first follow-up, and the company explicitly prizes direct communication. Never claim B2B-SaaS product tenure or "product designer" years you don't have. Your honest reframe ("different label, same substance, here's the working proof") is not a fallback; with him it's the stronger play.
2. **Hand-wavy AI talk.** "I'm very AI-native" with no toolchain is the kind of claim an engineer-exec will test hard; his public hiring posts emphasize real, demonstrated AI fluency. Give the specific pipeline, the specific decisions.
3. **Design-as-decoration.** If you talk aesthetics without workflow/business rationale, you lose the engineer-exec. He said it himself: design should "meaningfully shape how the business actually runs."
4. **Faking the domain.** He built FieldRoutes and ServiceTitan, he knows the trades buyer better than almost anyone. Do **not** perform abatement expertise. Show respect for the field → office → finance complexity, lean on your real "I get fluent in new industries fast" track record, and have a credible learn-first plan.
5. **Signaling slowness.** "I'd research for a month before touching anything" is the wrong music for a man who repeats "hours and days." Bias to action, which is true for you, is the right note.

## 9. The make-or-break moments unique to Atul (his angle + your honest answer)

Your strategy doc has the full Q&A bank. These four are the ones an engineer-turned-CPO uniquely creates. Rehearse these cold.

**A. The feasibility probe (his engineering core).**
He may ask: "You've never worked alongside an engineering team. How do you know what's actually buildable, and how do you hand off so design isn't thrown over a wall?"
> "Honestly, because I build my own designs in code. My pipeline is Claude Code, not just a static comp, so I'm designing against what's real, not a picture of what I wish were real. When I hand off, I can hand off a working prototype and the reasoning behind it, not a redline and good luck. I'd rather prototype the thing with your engineers than spec it at them."

**B. The AI-UX rigor probe (his DISCO / Olivia / ServiceTitan lineage).**
He may ask: "When the AI is wrong in the field, a bad estimate, a misread drawing, what does your design do?"
> "That's the real design problem, not the happy path. In my review tool I split the deterministic, transparent signal from the model's qualitative read and labeled which is which, so the user always knows what's a hard fact versus a judgment call. When the model can't be reached it fails honestly, tells the user and falls back, instead of pretending. And I kept a human in the loop so they can interrogate a result, not just trust it. Same principles I'd bring to a field user accepting or rejecting an AI suggestion, confidence shown, easy to override, never silently wrong."

**C. The velocity/ownership proof (his "hours and days").**
He may ask: "Walk me through something you took from nothing to shipped, fast, by yourself."
> "The Applicant Review Tool, for this role. Idea to live, working product in a few hours: concept and PRD in Cowork, build in Claude Code, versioned in GitHub, deployed. No team, no hand-holding. That's not a one-off; it's how I work now."

**D. The investment-tradeoff question (how an exec frames the bet).**
He may ask: "I've run product teams for twenty years. On paper you're a career-changer making a senior leap. Why is that a bet worth making?"
> "Because the thing that's hardest to hire for, I already am, someone who designs and ships AI-native, end to end, in hours. Most senior product designers are retrofitting AI into their process; I started there. The product-design vocabulary and the SaaS reps are the catch-up, and I ramp fast because I build to learn. I'd rather show you I'm productive in week one than claim a credential I don't have. Give me a real FieldFlō workflow and I'll prove it in an afternoon."
