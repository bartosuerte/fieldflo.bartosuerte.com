# FieldFlō Company Profile

Last updated: 2026-06-26. Maintained for interview prep.
Ground rule: accuracy over hype. Marketing and legal claims are flagged as such so they do not get repeated as established fact.

## Snapshot

- Category: AI-native vertical SaaS for specialty subcontractors. An all-in-one field-and-office management platform.
- Legal entity: The Maker System, LLC (the copyright holder on the marketing site). FieldFlō is the product brand.
- Backing: Mainsail Partners (growth equity).
- Origin: built by former subcontractors out of the abatement / demolition sector, frustrated with whiteboards, spreadsheets, and overpriced generic SaaS quotes.
- Tagline, used everywhere: "Built by contractors for contractors." Recurring CTA: "Don't rely on the status quo."
- Verticals marketed today: Demolition, Asbestos Abatement, Environmental Remediation, Masonry, Concrete.
- Marketing stack: the public site runs on HubSpot (CMS, forms, blog). Note: this is their marketing site, separate from the product app.

## Mission and origin

FieldFlō was started by subcontractors solving their own problem: too much time lost to mundane, repetitive admin (time tracking, safety paperwork, crew management, inventory) that, when dropped, causes wasted time, higher labor cost, and regulatory penalties. They built in-house after outsourced SaaS quotes came back too high and too generic, and after deciding digitized forms alone were not enough.

Stated design north star: accessibility. The system is meant to "transcend age, language, education, and technical expertise" so anyone on a crew can use it. This is the single most useful line for a product-design interview, because it is their own articulated design philosophy. Coming from an industry "accustomed to whiteboards and spreadsheets," change-resistance was a known constraint they designed against.

## Who they serve: five verticals, one origin

The product is DA&R-native and expanding. The original wedge was Demolition, Asbestos Abatement, and Environmental Remediation (DA&R). Masonry and Concrete are newer go-to-market expansions into adjacent self-perform trades that share the same operational DNA: field crews, heavy safety and compliance paperwork, time-and-material billing, certifications, and job costing.

Strong evidence the product was built asbestos / demolition first: every vertical page links to the same feature set, and the feature URLs are all prefixed "asbestos-demolition-" or "asbestos-" (for example asbestos-demolition-job-costing, asbestos-warehouse-inventory). The Masonry and Concrete pages even still carry demolition-specific copy in places (the Project Management blurb reads "across all your demolition projects" and the Form Builder blurb says "for your demolition business"). Read that as a recency signal: the expansion verticals are bolted onto a DA&R core and not yet fully localized.

Adjacent trades named in their own materials beyond the five pages: electrical, flooring, plastering, steel, and general trade / construction contractors.

## The platform: one product, roughly 13 modules

The identical feature suite appears on all five vertical pages, which confirms this is one horizontal platform sold into multiple verticals rather than separate products. The modules:

1. Project Management. Manage the day in the field across all projects; view documentation, certificates, and more.
2. Safety Management System. Track and manage every safety component of employees and projects from one dashboard.
3. Dashboard / Reports. Metrics at a glance, with deeper pull-down reporting.
4. Time and Material (T&M) Tracking. Automated T&M, notifications, and automated change orders.
5. CRM. Track and support sales efforts.
6. Time Tracking & Payroll. Employee and asset time, per diem, breaks, SMS sign-out. This is the FlōTime capability surfaced inside the platform.
7. Inventory / Equipment Warehouse Tracking. Inventory, equipment rentals, and more.
8. Online Training & Certificate Tracking. Build trainings, auto-create and upload certs.
9. Employee HR & Skill Tracking. Incidents, skills, roles, certs.
10. Scheduling System. Interactive scheduling with SMS deployment that auto-populates timesheets from the schedule.
11. Job Costing Tracking. Upload estimates; see bids vs. actuals in real time.
12. Form Builder. Custom forms.
13. Document Management. One organized place for documents.

The throughline across modules is consolidation: replace a stack of point tools and paper with one system that connects the field to the back office.

Two testimonials are worth knowing verbatim-ish:

- Asbestos (Kacey, Spray Systems): FieldFlō helped defend against a fraudulent injury claim. The worker had verified, every day, that they were not injured at the start and end of work. When shown that data, the claim was dropped. This is the sharpest real-world illustration of the "documentation as legal protection" value, the same CYA theme as the FlōTime time-tracking pitch.

He now starts his morning with the foreman's daily field report, auto-sent when the foreman closes their day. This is the consolidation play and the office-to-field reporting loop in one quote.

## Integrations and savings (the back-office wedge)

FieldFlō positions as the field system of record that feeds back-office accounting and ERP, rather than trying to replace them. Published integrations, all construction finance and ERP systems:

- Sage 100 Contractor, Sage 300 CRE, Sage Intacct
- QuickBooks Desktop, QuickBooks Enterprise, QuickBooks Online
- Acumatica
- Viewpoint Vista (Trimble)
- Foundation
- COINS
- Plus "additional integrations available via export."

Why this matters: it is a deliberate adoption wedge. FieldFlō owns the field-to-office data (time, T&M, job costing, certs, safety) and hands clean records to whatever accounting system the contractor already runs. It does not ask them to rip out Sage or QuickBooks. Contrast with a competitor like Plexxis that bundles its own accounting.

ROI claim: the /savings page reports an average of roughly $79K in annual cost savings, based on case studies across about 1,000 projects during their beta program. Treat the number as a marketing claim from a beta cohort, not an audited figure. It is their headline economic argument and worth knowing.

## The "Flō" product suite

FlōTime is FieldFlō's purpose-built mobile time-tracking app for field workers, the first publicly named product in a "Flō" branded suite, and it maps to the platform's Time Tracking & Payroll module. Source: FieldFlō blog, "Field Worker Time Tracking Is Getting Harder to Ignore. Meet FlōTime!" (2026-05-27).

Mechanics: text-invite onboarding (no app store, no email, no password; sub-10-second sign-in), GPS-verified clock-in, auto-enforced meal and break compliance, clock-out face-photo verification (positioned as the kill-switch for buddy punching), and payroll-ready sync to FieldFlō.

Why now, per FieldFlō: field-worker time-tracking laws are tightening, with California leading and New York, Illinois, and Michigan following, and possible federal alignment within three to five years. Treat this as their marketing and legal narrative. Verify before repeating it as fact.

Two-sided value: the company gets compliance, CYA documentation, and operational visibility; the worker gets first-time access to their own records and the ability to catch errors before payroll closes.

## Competitive landscape

For the full breakdown, competitor cards, the design-and-UX angle, and head-to-head soundbites, see the companion doc "FieldFlo Competitive Battlecard.md". Summary below.

FieldFlō sits at an unusual intersection: vertically specialized (DA&R) and broad (all-in-one). Most rivals are one or the other. Four buckets:

1. DA&R and abatement-specific (closest niche rivals). Ecesis (asbestos management and AHERA compliance, leans EHS and building-owner side), Projul (demolition contractor software, flat-rate pricing around $4,788 per year with no per-user fees, full hazmat-to-clearance workflow), BuildStackHub (demolition estimating, material salvage, environmental compliance), EasyBOP (UK asbestos contract management). FieldFlō is generally broader operationally than these, several of which are compliance or estimating point tools.

2. Subcontractor all-in-one platforms (overlap on "built for subs"). Knowify (job costing and PM for commercial subs), eSUB (PM and document control for subs), Plexxis (PM plus accounting, estimating, takeoff on one stack; strong in wall and ceiling), BuildOps (commercial specialty, strong in mechanical, electrical, HVAC service), Bizns Tool (specialty trades). These compete on breadth but are not DA&R-specialized.

3. General construction PM platforms (top-down, broad). Procore is the giant, connecting owners, GCs, and specialty contractors with a very large suite, often too broad and too expensive for a specialty sub, which is exactly the gap FieldFlō targets. Also Autodesk Construction Cloud, Oracle Primavera, Viewpoint. Note Viewpoint Vista is also a FieldFlō integration partner, so that relationship is partly complementary.

4. Field time-tracking and daily-report point tools (overlap with FlōTime only). Busybusy (whole-crew real-time tracking, low per-user, free tier), Raken (daily reports plus time, supervisor-priced, GC-leaning), Rhumbix (time and production tracking), Workyard (GPS accuracy and job costing, roughly $6 to $13 per user per month plus a base fee), ExakTime, ClockShark. These rival the FlōTime module, not the platform. FieldFlō's counter: time tracking is one module in an all-in-one, so a contractor avoids stitching a time app to separate forms, certs, and job-costing tools (the Winegardner two-tools-to-one story).

How to talk about differentiation:

- The wedge is "specialized and consolidated." Procore is broad but generic and pricey; point tools are cheap but narrow; niche compliance tools are deep but thin on operations. FieldFlō is the one built for DA&R that also runs the whole field-to-office workflow.
- "Built by contractors, affordable" is both origin story and positioning against Procore-class cost and complexity.
- Integrate, do not replace, on accounting is a smart, low-friction adoption play.
- Watch-items: BuildOps (AI-native, about $1B valuation, now expanding from commercial service trades into specialty-contractor project management) is the best-funded adjacent threat; plus Procore moving down-market, point tools undercutting on price for a single workflow, and niche tools going deeper on AHERA-style compliance. FieldFlō's defense is breadth plus DA&R vertical fit plus price.

## Strategic read (for the interview)

- Multi-vertical expansion on a horizontal product. The design challenge is keeping one platform coherent while serving five-plus trades with different workflows, safety regimes, and language. The recency of Masonry and Concrete (and the unlocalized copy) means there is live, unfinished design work here. Good thing to ask about: how the team handles vertical-specific configuration (forms, certs, safety templates) without forking the product.
- Vertical marketing, horizontal engine. The features are identical; only the story changes. That implies the product needs strong template and configuration systems, not separate builds. A designer who can talk about scalable, configurable systems fits this.
- Compliance and safety as the wedge. Asbestos is the most regulated entry point, and the injury-claim testimonial shows documentation acting as legal defense. Expect compliance, auditability, and "defensible records" to be recurring product themes.
- Consolidation is the core promise. The pitch is killing point tools and paper. The Winegardner quote (two tools to one) is the proof. Frame your work as reducing tool sprawl and connecting field to office.
- Dual-persona, field-first. A back-office / compliance buyer and a non-technical field-worker user with opposite needs, used in poor connectivity, with gloves, glare, and language diversity. The "text invite, no password, 10-second sign-in" principle is the clearest expression of designing for the field user.
- Handle the copy quirk with care. The unlocalized demolition copy on the masonry and concrete pages is a real attention-to-detail catch, but leading with "your website has bugs" in an interview is risky. Better as a thoughtful observation about expansion stage if the conversation invites it.

## Culture and hiring

From the careers page, FieldFlō describes an "international team" that is "scrappy, driven, and full of heart," that rolls up its sleeves, takes ownership, and delivers real results, with the lines "excellence doesn't have to be boring" and "your ideas matter, your impact is visible, and your teammates have your back." The mission framing is "shape the future of construction tech." Open roles are posted on a Gusto job board. Read it as a small, high-agency startup that wants builders who own outcomes.

For Barton: that culture maps cleanly onto the self-built application artifacts. Building the review tool and interactive resume end to end, solo, with an AI workflow, is concrete evidence of the scrappy, take-ownership profile they describe. Worth foregrounding, honestly framed.
